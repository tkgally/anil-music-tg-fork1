Five studio prototypes

Background music for working brains — five different ways to generate, and to let a listener shape, music that stays out of the way while you code.

Built for the Daysong collaboration: they take Daysong's premise — a whole piece composed and synthesized in the browser, no samples, no server — and ask what the studio becomes when the goal is music for the background of a workday instead of music for listening. Two ideas run through all five: two-level controls (a few plain-English sliders first, the full set behind “Advanced”) and background-first defaults (melody scarce and low, midrange and bass forward, generous repetition, evolution one small step at a time). Everything is live — there is no “generate” button — and everything is deterministic: the default seed is today's date, and the URL pins the exact piece, so you can share what you're hearing as a link.

How to listen: press play, then go do something else for ten minutes — these are meant to be judged from the corner of your ear, not head-on. Then come back and try the preset chips, push the big slider to both ends, and open Advanced. Each page explains what its engine is doing in a one-line status readout and a small visualization.

The prototypes

01
Tides — drones that breathe
A just-intoned drone chord over a fixed pedal, swelling like the sea; harmony drifts one voice at a time.
Explores: the zero-melody extreme · wave motion from three slow, unsynced LFOs · harmonic change as weather (35-second chord glides) · a “voice-masking” texture option. Headline control: Depth (dark ↔ bright).
02
Drift — loops out of phase
A few soft tones, each repeating on its own slow clock — Eno’s Music for Airports trick as an editable instrument.
Explores: repetition without staleness (every element repeats; only the alignment evolves) · a layer table as the “studio” (edit note / period / pan per loop) · a live “pattern repeats in ≈ N days” readout. Headline control: Pace.
03
Pulse — patterns that evolve one note at a time
A soft clockwork ostinato, played by warm keys and their octave-down canon, that rewrites itself one cell at a time.
Explores: gradual evolution made literal (one seeded mutation every ~25 s — watch it happen in the ring) · pulse without drums · freeze / nudge controls over the evolution itself. Headline control: Energy.
04
Dust — lo-fi beats to code to
The coffee-shop classic, fully synthesized: lazy swung drums, dark jazz chords, vinyl crackle and rain — and no melody unless you ask.
Explores: the genre coders actually play · lo-fi as subtraction (lowpass everything, no fills, no hook) · texture layers (dust, rain) as first-class controls. Headline control: Dust.
05
Deep Work — Daysong, retuned for the background
Anil’s engine — same chord walk, same voices, same motif logic — folded behind one “presence” slider, with a work-session arc.
Explores: how the existing Daysong parameter space collapses into background ↔ foreground (the mapping table is shown live in Advanced) · session shapes (25/50 min with a wind-down and a soft chime) · an optional, honestly-labeled 16 Hz “focus pulse”. Headline control: Presence.

Where each sits

TidesDrift Deep WorkPulse Dust
least stimulating — deep, difficult work more stimulating — routine, automatized work

The research is fairly clear that this axis should be the listener’s choice: complex work wants near-textural sound; routine work tolerates (and is often helped by) more rhythm and event density. Deep Work’s presence slider moves along this axis inside a single prototype.

The design brief, distilled

  • Steady-state beats changing-state. Sound that varies unpredictably from moment to moment (speech, salient melodies, dramatic entrances) reliably captures attention; repetitive, predictable sound does not. So: sustained textures, slow crossfades, high repetition, and evolution in single small steps spaced tens of seconds apart.
  • Melody is the risk, register is the multiplier. Isolated high notes read as “deviants” and trigger orienting. Defaults here keep melodic content sparse, soft-attacked, and capped around C5, with the mix centered on bass and midrange.
  • No voices, nothing voice-like. Lyrics are the most robust distractor for people who work in language (and code is language). Even vowel-like formant timbres are avoided by default.
  • Dynamics breathe, never jump. A few dB of slow swell reads as alive; a sudden level or brightness change reads as an event. Everything here is compressed gently and fades in over seconds.
  • Repetition is a feature; surprise is a bug. The brain stops orienting to what it can predict. Each prototype repeats generously and changes one thing at a time — the five differ in what repeats (a drone, a loop, a pattern, a groove, a motif).
  • Match stimulation to the task, per listener. Effects differ hugely between people (and tasks); the honest design is a quiet default plus a clearly-labeled way to dial stimulation up or down — not a claim that one setting boosts anyone’s brain.

Trying them

Everything runs right here in the browser — no accounts, no network, no audio files; every sound is synthesized on the spot with the Web Audio API. The same folder also runs locally: download studio-prototypes/ from the repo and double-click any index.html (plain files, zero dependencies — file:// works). space toggles play. Reroll the seed for a new piece; share the URL to share the exact one. tools/verify.mjs is the objective “listening test” used while building these — a headless browser plays each prototype and measures spectral centroid, level stability, onset salience, high-frequency share, and silence gaps; every prototype ships passing its targets, and each was independently adversarially reviewed (details in README.md).
